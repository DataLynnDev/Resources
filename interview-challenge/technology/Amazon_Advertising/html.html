# Filter out outliers

%%sql
WITH CombinedAds AS (
    -- Joining both tables on 'product_id'
    SELECT
        a.ad_id,
        a.product_id,
        a.click_count,
        a.view_count,
        a.purchase_count,
        a.date,
        b.advertiser_id,
        b.budget,
        b.campaign_start,
        b.campaign_end
    FROM AdsPerformance a
    JOIN AdvertiserInfo b ON a.product_id = b.product_id
)

-- Top 5 advertisers by click-to-view ratio
, ClickToViewRatio AS (
    SELECT
        advertiser_id,
        budget,
        SUM(click_count) / NULLIF(SUM(view_count), 0) AS click_view_ratio
    FROM CombinedAds
    GROUP BY advertiser_id, budget
    ORDER BY click_view_ratio DESC
    LIMIT 5
)

-- Top 5 advertisers by purchase rate
, PurchaseRate AS (
    SELECT
        advertiser_id,
        budget,
        SUM(purchase_count) / NULLIF(SUM(click_count), 0) AS purchase_rate
    FROM CombinedAds
    GROUP BY advertiser_id, budget
    ORDER BY purchase_rate DESC
    LIMIT 5
)

-- Combine results using UNION
SELECT * FROM ClickToViewRatio
UNION ALL
SELECT * FROM PurchaseRate;

Question 3¶

Scenario: There's a hypothesis in the team that ads with a higher budget generally have a higher purchase rate (purchase_count/click_count). You've decided to test this hypothesis using the data at hand.

Question: State the null and alternative hypotheses for this scenario. Calculate the correlation coefficient between the 'budget' from AdvertiserInfo and the purchase rate from AdsPerformance. Implement this calculation in a coding language of your choice and interpret the results.

Null Hypothesis (H): There is no correlation between the ad budget and the purchase rate, i.e., an increased budget does not influence the purchase rate.

Alternative Hypothesis (H): There is a significant correlation between the ad budget and the purchase rate, i.e., ads with a higher budget generally have a higher purchase rate.

In [ ]:
# Merge the two dataframes on product_id

dfi = df_AdvertiserInfo.copy()
dfi['product_id'] = dfi['product_id'].astype(str)
combined_data = pd.merge(dfp, dfi, on='product_id')

# Calculate purchase rate
combined_data['purchase_rate'] = combined_data['purchase_count'] / combined_data['click_count']

# Calculate the correlation coefficient
correlation = combined_data['budget'].corr(combined_data['purchase_rate'])

print(correlation)

The result correlation is very close to 0. This means the budget does not influence the purchase rate and we fail to reject the Null Hypothesis.

Question 4¶

Scenario: Recently, a particular product's actual revenue has been observed to be 40% lower than its predicted revenue from the ads.

Question: Based on the data from the AdsPerformance and AdvertiserInfo tables, identify potential reasons for this gap in predicted and actual revenue. Additionally, how would you modify the ad strategy or placement to ensure a more accurate sales forecast for future campaigns?

Potential Reasons in Data:

  • Ad Click-Through vs. Actual Purchase: The click_count might be high, indicating interest, but purchase_count may not align. If many users click the ad but don't purchase the product, it suggests there's interest but a conversion issue.
  • Ad Views vs. Clicks: A high view_count with a lower click_count can indicate that the ad isn't enticing enough to get users to engage with it.
  • Budget and Ad Placement: While budget provides an indication of how much was spent, it doesn't provide a clear picture of where the ad was placed. If an ad had a high budget but was placed in less-optimal locations or times, this could impact its effectiveness.
  • External Factors: These tables don't capture other potential factors such as pricing, competitors' ads, product availability, market conditions, or user reviews which can greatly influence the purchase decision.

Modifications & Strategy:

  • Retargeting: For users who clicked on the ad but didn't make a purchase, consider retargeting campaigns. This involves showing ads specifically to these users, sometimes offering discounts or highlighting product features.
  • Optimize Ad Placement and Timing: Analyze when and where the most successful ads (in terms of conversion) are placed. Adjust your strategy to favor these high-performing slots.
  • A/B Testing: Experiment with different ad creatives, placements, or messaging to see which version gets better results. Analyze the data to understand which version is more effective.
  • Align Predictive Metrics: Instead of just looking at clicks, consider using more reliable predictive metrics such as the conversion rate or cost per conversion to make sales predictions.
