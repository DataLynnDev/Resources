def segment_users(row):
    if row['Avg_Steps'] > step_threshold and row['Total_Duration'] > duration_threshold:
        return 'High Steps & High Duration'
    elif row['Avg_Steps'] > step_threshold and row['Total_Duration'] <= duration_threshold:
        return 'High Steps & Low Duration'
    elif row['Avg_Steps'] <= step_threshold and row['Total_Duration'] > duration_threshold:
        return 'Low Steps & High Duration'
    else:
        return 'Low Steps & Low Duration'

user_summary['Segment'] = user_summary.apply(segment_users, axis=1)

# The segment_users function assigns users to one of four categories, based on their average steps and total duration.
print("\nUser Segment Counts:")

for segment in user_summary['Segment'].unique():

Question 2¶

Scenario: Apple wants to improve its Health app by tailoring features to different types of users. Understanding sleep patterns is essential to make app recommendations and improve user health.

Question: Compute the average SleepHours for users participating in clinical studies (StudyParticipation = yes) versus those who do not. Using statistical tests, determine if there's a significant difference in sleep patterns between these two groups.

Solution¶

Hints:¶

  1. Calculate Average SleepHours:

    • Filter data based on StudyParticipation status (yes/no).
    • Compute the mean of SleepHours for both groups.

  2. Statistical Test for Difference:

    • We can use the Independent Samples t-test to compare the means of the two groups.
    • The null hypothesis (H0) will be that there's no difference in sleep patterns between the two groups. The alternative hypothesis (H1) will be that there's a significant difference.

  3. Interpretation:

    • Interpret the results of the statistical test.
    • Comment on any significant findings.

Step 1: Calculate Average SleepHours:¶

Step 2: Statistical Test for Difference:¶

In [ ]:
from scipy.stats import ttest_ind

## To determine if there's a significant difference in sleep patterns between the two groups, we'll use the Independent Samples t-test.
# Filter data for both groups
study_sleep = health_data[health_data['StudyParticipation'] == 'yes']['SleepHours']
non_study_sleep = health_data[health_data['StudyParticipation'] == 'no']['SleepHours']

# Conduct t-test
t_stat, p_value = ttest_ind(study_sleep, non_study_sleep)

print("T-statistic:", t_stat)
print("P-value:", p_value)

T-statistic: 0.8375326434913098
P-value: 0.40249375606776405

Step 3: Interpretation:¶

In [ ]:
alpha = 0.05  # Common significance level (confidence interval - 95%)
if p_value < alpha:
    print("There's a significant difference in sleep patterns between the study and non-study groups.")
else:
    print("There's no significant difference in sleep patterns between the study and non-study groups.")

There's no significant difference in sleep patterns between the study and non-study groups.

Note: If the p-value is less than our significance level (e.g., 0.05), then we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is indeed a significant difference in sleep patterns between users who participate in studies and those who don't. Otherwise, we would accept the null hypothesis.

Comment

The lack of significant difference suggests that participation in clinical studies (as recorded in the Health app) doesn't notably affect the sleep patterns of the users.

This can mean a couple of things:

  • The studies they're participating in might not be related to sleep or affecting their sleep in any significant manner.
  • The Health app's influence or any recommendations provided might not be altering sleep behavior for those in studies compared to those not in studies.

Question 3¶

Scenario: A new version of the Health app was recently released. Apple wants to determine if the app update increased users' participation in clinical studies.

Question: Conduct an A/B test where Group A comprises users on the old version and Group B includes users on the new version. Using the StudyParticipation column, determine if there's a significant increase in study participation in Group B.

Solution¶

1. Experiment Design:¶

A/B Testing:¶

  • Control Group (A): Users on the old version of the Health app.
  • Treatment Group (B): Users on the new version of the Health app.

Randomly assign users to these groups, ensuring even distribution to prevent any biases.

2. Metrics to Measure:¶

  • Primary Metric: Study Participation Rate. This will indicate whether users on the new app version are more inclined to take part in clinical studies.
  • Secondary Metrics:
    • Activity: Types and frequency of activities logged, to understand if users are more active in one group.
    • Engagement: This can be measured by other metrics such as heart rate, steps, calories, etc., to see if users are engaging more with the app in one group.

3. Duration of the Experiment:¶

Considering the importance of the update and the need to gather a significant amount of data for conclusive results, the experiment should run for at least a few weeks or until a statistically significant number of users have participated.

4. Analysis:¶

After the data collection period, conduct a Chi-square test to compare the study participation rates between Groups A and B.

  • If Group B shows a statistically significant higher participation rate than Group A, this suggests the new app version encourages more users to participate in clinical studies.

5. Other Considerations:¶

  • Feedback Review: Going beyond numbers, analyze any user feedback regarding the new features or changes in the app. This might give insights into why there's an increase or lack thereof in study participation.
  • Segment Analysis: It's beneficial to analyze the behavior of various user segments - for instance, users who frequently log activities vs. those who don't. Such segments might have different responses to the app update.
  • Ensure Stability: Guarantee that no other significant updates or changes are introduced during the test to maintain the experiment's integrity.

6. Conclusion:¶

Should the data indicate that the new app version led to increased clinical study participation, then Apple can confidently roll out the update to all users. However, if there's no significant difference, or if participation decreases, Apple might need to revisit the app's features or the manner they're presented. Regardless of the outcome, continue to monitor user behavior and feedback to ensure sustained positive impact.
